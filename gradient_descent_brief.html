01
The Big Picture

The Mountain Analogy

Imagine you are standing somewhere on a foggy mountain at night. You want to reach the lowest valley — but you can't see far ahead. What do you do?

You feel the slope beneath your feet, and take a small step downhill. Repeat until you can't go any lower.

That's gradient descent in one sentence: repeatedly take small steps in the direction that reduces cost, until you reach the minimum.

Key insight: We always move opposite to the gradient direction. The gradient points uphill — so we subtract it to go downhill.
02
What We're Minimizing

The Cost Function

Before we can descend, we need something to descend on. The cost function J(θ) measures how wrong our model is — it converts all the errors across the entire dataset into a single number.

Mean Squared Error — The Classic Choice

For linear regression, we use MSE:

J(θ) = (1/2n) · Σ (ŷᵢ − yᵢ)²

Where ŷᵢ is the predicted value, yᵢ is the actual value, and n is the number of data points. The ½ is just there to make the derivative cleaner.

Visualising the Cost Landscape

Click anywhere on the canvas below to place the model at that cost level. The goal is always to reach the bottom.

Why not just pick the lowest point directly? In real models, cost functions have hundreds or thousands of dimensions (one per parameter). We can't visualise or analytically solve them — we need an iterative algorithm.

03
Direction Finder

The Gradient

The gradient is the slope of the cost function at your current position. It's the first derivative — how much the cost changes when you tweak a parameter slightly.

First Derivative — A Refresher

For a function f(w), the derivative f'(w) tells you the slope at point w:

f'(w) = df/dw = lim[Δw→0] (f(w+Δw) − f(w)) / Δw

Move your mouse over the curve to see the tangent (gradient) at each point

Positive vs. Negative Gradient

Positive Gradient
Slope goes upward → we are to the RIGHT of the minimum → step LEFT (subtract)
Negative Gradient
Slope goes downward → we are to the LEFT of the minimum → step RIGHT (subtract a negative = add)
Either way, subtracting the gradient always moves us toward the minimum. This is why the update rule uses a minus sign.

The Core Equation

The Update Rule

Everything in gradient descent collapses into one equation, applied once per iteration:

θnewparameter (new)
=
θparameter (old)
−
αlearning rate
·
∂J/∂θgradient of cost

For Linear Regression (m and b)

Our model is ŷ = mx + b. We have two parameters to update simultaneously:

m = m − α · (∂J/∂m)
b = b − α · (∂J/∂b)
⚠ Important: Both parameters must be updated simultaneously using the gradients computed at the current values. Don't update m first and then use the new m to compute the gradient for b!

The Iteration Process

  • 1.Initialize parameters (e.g., m = 0, b = 0)
  • 2.Compute predictions ŷ = mx + b for all data points
  • 3.Compute cost J = (1/2n)·Σ(ŷᵢ − yᵢ)²
  • 4.Compute gradients ∂J/∂m and ∂J/∂b
  • 5.Update: m ← m − α·∂J/∂m and b ← b − α·∂J/∂b
  • 6.Repeat steps 2–5 until convergence (cost stops decreasing significantly)
05
The Critical Hyperparameter

Learning Rate α

The learning rate α controls how large each step is. It's the most important hyperparameter in gradient descent, and getting it wrong breaks the algorithm.

Rules of thumb for choosing α:

  • Start with α = 0.01 or 0.001 and adjust
  • If cost is oscillating or increasing, α is too large
  • If cost decreases but very slowly over thousands of iterations, try increasing α
  • Plot cost vs. iteration — it should decrease smoothly
